In recent years, I have particularly enjoyed working in teams and organisations who embrace an agile approach to delivering software. In the best ones, I've felt everyone is open with each other, and all focussed towards creating products effectively. 

This period has coincided with a better awareness of my mental state and methods to maintain and improve my well being. Recently, I've noticed a few similarities between having a smoothly operating team, and maintaining a healthy mental state day-to-day - some are a little tenuous!

**DISCLAIMER** - I am not an expert (I'm not even an expert in myself)! Please talk to a trusted friend and/or your GP if you have persistent problems.

## Agility/Flexibility

### In Software

To me, being agile in software is enabling the team to react quickly and safely to change; be it new features or fixing bugs. This is opposed to "big bang" projects that drag on and end up being unfit for purpose when they finally get released; or simple updates being fraught with anxiety, where each release is susceptible unknown side effects or human errors in the release process.

Being agile looks to reduce the feedback time on development, helping the team deliver useful, working software as soon as possible. We can employ a number of practices to help us achieve this, and improve upon over time.

### For You

Improving one's psychological flexibility allows us to handle stressful issues rationally, and respond in a manner that is aligned to our needs. Again, practices can be employed to improve a rigid way of thinking.

## Monitoring

### In Software

When an application is deployed, we need tools to keep an eye on things and let us know when and if it begins to operate in an unexpected way. We can also use the data to measure the efficacy of released features; as well as using that data to steer us towards new features.

### For You

To help us monitor our well-being, we can practise being mindful of what we experience throughout the day; noticing actions and thoughts that initially happen on auto-pilot. We can start with noticing small things like how the body moves whilst we breathe, or our surroundings as we walk to work, and then move towards noticing how the body reacts during particular situations.

## Root Cause Analysis

### In Software

When a major issue occurs - such as a database accidentally getting wiped - after fixing the issue, investigating how it happened, beyond the initial problem, helps us reduce the chance of it occurring it again. One technique that can be employed is "Five Whys". This is where the team start with the the immediate cause: "Dave ran his tests in production". Then the team keep asking themselves how/why that happened: "we need to run tests manually, and I forgot to change the config" or "we all have production database keys because we have to write schema changes manually". After five iterations, the team will usually come to some solutions that will prevent the issue happening again, in this case: testing environments, continuous integration, and database migrations.

### For You

Unfavourable responses to stressful situations are often the automated result of years of baked-in behaviour. Through mindfulness, we can begin to notice the automatic physiological response to a particular situation and the initial mental reaction from it. From there, we can practise riding this initial urge to respond in our usual way, and consider how we would prefer to act.

** NOTE ** Investigating the root causes of the baked-in behaviour requires a truly safe space, and probably the help of a trained professional!

## Systems Thinking

### In Software

In a non-agile team, we often see each discipline involved being treated as separate processes. The product owner creates some features with the client, the designers design how it will work, the front-end developers build the interface, and the back-end developers integrate it all with the rest of the system. Then it all gets thrown over the fence to the testers who find an issue and its sent back to the start to fix a fundamental issue.

Considering the delivery of features as a system as a whole breaks down this siloed working, improves communication between team members and reduces bottlenecks; ultimately delivering new work quicker and returning value earlier. This thinking can be expanded over entire businesses and services to help produce more effective solutions for teams, and ultimately, the people who use their product.

### For You

Automated mental reactions are in response to physical triggers in the body, so its important to keep the physical body in check, too. Regular exercise keeps the body fit and responsive, as well as another opportunity to practise mindfulness eg yoga, or my new discovery: weight training. Over-indulging in alcohol, coffee or other drugs is inevitably going to affect your internal chemical balance.

## Prioritisation of Actions

### In Software

When faced with a hundred things to do, features should be prioritised towards what will deliver the most value to the user. This ensures effort is not wasted on pointless work, and we get a return on effort as soon as possible.

### For You

With the ability to notice and gently resist the automated reaction, we can start to move towards actions that we would prefer. By identifying our core values or qualities that are important to us, we can move towards actions that embody and fulfil these values.

## YAGNI

### In Software

When developing software products, it is tempting to build a load of new features at once, or to optimise prematurely, or re-factor to a pattern straight away. Often, this is a waste of time. Those additional features never get used; the service you replicated over a new service bus you setup doesn't get the traffic you thought it would; the code you re-factored doesn't work with this other thing that needs to be considered. In most cases, "You Ain't Gonna Need It".

### For You

YAGNI is attempting to predict the future, so with thoughts we should try to keep in the present ie not dwelling in the past or catastrophising about what might happen. Post Traumatic Stress Disorder is the mind and body literally reliving the past event, unable to move on.

## Ease of Change

### In Software

Unlike buildings and other fields of engineering, software is relatively easy to change and update. Some of the practices above enable us to improve the team and improve the code to make these changes even easier.

### For You

Growing up in a good environment helps children form psychological flexibility, wherein they are able to cope with day-to-day stresses. A lack of this environment in early years, or trauma in later life stunts this development and manifests itself in mental health issues. Fortunately, the brain is plastic and can be re-wired with practice.
